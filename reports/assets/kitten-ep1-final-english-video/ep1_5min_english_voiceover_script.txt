Hey friends, welcome to Kitten Economics, the show where tiny kittens make big economic ideas easy to understand.

Today, Momo the kitten is going to teach us one of the most useful ideas in everyday life: opportunity cost.

At first, opportunity cost sounds like a serious economics term. But the idea is actually very simple. Every time you choose one thing, you are also giving up something else. The best thing you give up is your opportunity cost.

Look at Momo. Momo sees two things at the same time. On the left, there is a delicious dried fish snack. On the right, there is a fun feather toy. Momo wants both. But right now, Momo can only choose one first.

If Momo chooses the fish, Momo gets the snack. That sounds good. But the opportunity cost is the fun playtime Momo gives up at that moment.

If Momo chooses the feather toy, Momo gets to play. That also sounds good. But the opportunity cost is the fish snack Momo gives up for now.

So opportunity cost is not just the money you spend. It is the value of the best alternative you did not choose.

This is why opportunity cost matters. It helps us see the hidden price of our decisions. Sometimes the real cost is not written on a price tag. Sometimes the real cost is time, energy, attention, or a better choice we missed.

Let’s try another kitten example. Momo is sitting beside a sunny window. The floor is warm. The room is quiet. It is the perfect time for a nap.

But nearby, there is a puzzle feeder. If Momo plays with it, Momo can learn, think, and maybe become a smarter little kitten.

If Momo naps now, the opportunity cost is learning time. If Momo trains with the puzzle now, the opportunity cost is comfort and rest.

Notice something important: economics does not always say one choice is right and the other is wrong. A nap can be a good choice if Momo is tired. Learning can be a good choice if Momo has energy. The smart choice depends on the goal.

Now let’s bring this idea into human life.

If you spend one hour scrolling on your phone, you are not only spending one hour. You are also giving up one hour that could have been used for sleep, exercise, study, work, or talking to someone you care about.

If you buy a new gadget today, the money is not the only cost. The opportunity cost might be the course you did not buy, the savings you did not keep, or the better tool you could have bought later.

If you say yes to every small request, the opportunity cost might be your focus. If you say yes to every distraction, the opportunity cost might be your progress.

That is why opportunity cost is powerful. It makes invisible trade-offs visible.

Here is Momo’s simple three-step method.

Step one: What am I choosing?

This sounds obvious, but many people skip it. Name the choice clearly. Am I choosing rest? Am I choosing entertainment? Am I choosing work? Am I choosing a purchase?

Step two: What am I giving up?

This is the heart of opportunity cost. Do not compare your choice with nothing. Compare it with the best realistic alternative.

Step three: Is it worth it in the long term?

Sometimes a fun choice is worth it. Sometimes rest is worth it. Sometimes spending money is worth it. But we want to choose on purpose, not by accident.

Now Momo has a smarter idea. Momo does not need to think only in either-or terms. Momo can play first, then eat the fish later. Or Momo can finish the puzzle first, then enjoy a nap.

The lesson is not that you must always choose the serious option. The lesson is that the order of choices matters. A better order can create a better outcome.

Opportunity cost is everywhere: in money, in time, in habits, in business, and in daily life.

Before your next decision, pause for three seconds and ask: What is the best thing I am giving up?

That one question can help you protect your time, spend money more wisely, and make choices that match your real goals.

So today’s lesson from Momo is simple: every choice has a hidden trade-off. Find it before you choose.
